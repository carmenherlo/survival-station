EMERGENCY FOOD: FORAGING, HUNTING, AND PRESERVATION

PRIORITIES
The human body can survive 3 weeks without food but only 3 days without water.
Food is a lower priority than water, shelter, and fire.
Digesting food requires water — do not eat if severely dehydrated.

THE UNIVERSAL EDIBILITY TEST
When unsure if a plant is safe, use this test. Takes 8 hours. Test one plant part at a time.
1. Separate the plant into parts: leaves, stem, root, flower, fruit.
2. Smell: strong unpleasant odors may indicate toxins.
3. Skin contact: rub on inner wrist. Wait 15 minutes for reaction.
4. Lip contact: touch to lips. Wait 3 minutes.
5. Mouth contact: place on tongue. Wait 15 minutes. Spit out.
6. Chew: chew a small amount without swallowing. Wait 15 minutes.
7. Swallow a small amount. Wait 8 hours. Eat nothing else during this time.
If no reaction occurs, the plant part is likely safe in moderate quantities.
NEVER eat plants with: milky sap (unless confirmed safe), beans/seeds in pods, bitter almond smell,
fine hairs or spines, three-leaf patterns (poison ivy), or white/yellow berries.

WIDELY SAFE EDIBLE PLANTS (NORTHERN HEMISPHERE)
- Dandelion (Taraxacum): entire plant edible. Leaves raw or cooked. Roots roasted as coffee substitute.
- Cattail (Typha): found near water. Young shoots, pollen, and rhizomes edible.
  Rhizomes can be processed into flour.
- Plantain (Plantago): common lawn weed. Leaves edible raw or cooked. Not banana plantain.
- Pine: inner bark (cambium) edible. Young needles for vitamin C tea. Pine nuts where available.
- Nettles (Urtica): cooking destroys sting. High in protein and iron. Use gloves to harvest.
- Acorns: all species edible after leaching. Boil in multiple changes of water to remove tannins.

INSECTS AS FOOD
High in protein and fat. Globally the most accessible emergency protein source.
- Safe to eat: grasshoppers, crickets, ants, beetle larvae, earthworms, termites.
- Remove wings and legs. Cook thoroughly to destroy parasites.
- Avoid: brightly colored insects (warning coloration), hairy insects, insects that sting.
- Earthworms: purge in clean water for a few hours, then boil.

FISHING
- Improvised hooks from wire, pins, thorns, or carved bone.
- Fish are most active at dawn and dusk.
- Look for fish in shaded areas, behind rocks, in bends of rivers.
- Improvised net: fine cloth or woven grass between two poles, swept through shallow water.
- Fish traps: funnel-shaped structures built from sticks in streams.

HUNTING AND TRAPPING
- Snares are more efficient than active hunting in survival situations.
- Place snares on animal trails, near burrows, or at natural funnels between obstacles.
- Simple loop snare: use wire or strong cord, loop about the size of a fist for rabbits.
  Position the loop at head height of the target animal, 10cm above the ground for rabbits.
- Check snares every 4-6 hours. Remove and move if no catch after 24 hours.
- All wild game must be cooked thoroughly to destroy parasites and bacteria.

FOOD PRESERVATION WITHOUT REFRIGERATION
- Drying/jerky: slice meat thin (3-5mm), remove fat, dry in sun and wind or low heat.
  Fully dried jerky lasts weeks. Fat causes rancidity.
- Smoking: cold smoke for flavor and drying, hot smoke (70°C+) for pathogen reduction.
  Combine with drying for longest preservation.
- Salt curing: pack meat or fish in dry salt. Draws out moisture.
  Requires significant salt quantities.
- Fermentation: controlled bacterial growth can preserve food (sauerkraut, kimchi principles).
  Requires knowledge to distinguish safe fermentation from spoilage.

CALORIC PRIORITIES
Fat: 9 kcal/g — most energy-dense. Prioritize fatty foods.
Carbohydrates: 4 kcal/g — fast energy. Roots, tubers, grains.
Protein: 4 kcal/g — essential for tissue repair but requires water to metabolize.
In cold environments, fat requirements increase significantly.
