EMERGENCY SHELTER: CONSTRUCTION AND PRINCIPLES

PRIORITIES
Shelter is the first survival priority when exposed to extreme temperatures, wind, or rain.
Hypothermia can occur even at temperatures above 10°C if conditions are wet and windy.
The human body loses heat 25 times faster in water than in air.

SITE SELECTION
- Avoid: valley floors (cold air settles), ridgelines (wind exposure), dry riverbeds (flash flood risk),
  lone trees (lightning), dead standing trees (widow-makers).
- Seek: natural windbreaks, southern exposure (northern hemisphere) for maximum sun,
  proximity to water (but not flood risk), flat dry ground.
- Check overhead for dead branches before settling.

INSULATION PRINCIPLES
- Dead air space insulates. Layers of leaves, grass, or pine needles trap air effectively.
- Ground insulation is critical: you lose more heat to the ground than to the air.
  Use at least 10cm of dry material beneath you.
- Small shelters retain body heat better than large ones.

DEBRIS HUT
Best emergency shelter requiring no tools or materials.
1. Find a ridgepole 3 meters long. Rest one end on a stump or fork at hip height.
2. Lean branches (ribs) along both sides at 45 degrees.
3. Cover ribs with smaller sticks, then pile leaves, grass, bark, or pine needles
   to a depth of at least 60cm — enough to keep out rain and insulate.
4. Fill the interior with dry leaves for bedding.
5. Block the entrance with a pile of leaves or a pack.
A well-built debris hut can retain warmth even in near-freezing conditions.

LEAN-TO
Faster to build, less insulating than a debris hut. Good for wind and light rain.
1. Tie a horizontal pole between two trees at shoulder height.
2. Lean branches against it at 45 degrees.
3. Layer bark, leaves, or a tarp over the frame.
4. Build a fire in front for heat reflection.

TARP SHELTER
If a tarp or emergency blanket is available:
- A-frame: tie a ridgeline between trees, drape tarp over it, stake edges to ground.
- Lean-to: attach one edge high, stake opposite edge low to the ground.
- Always angle the tarp to shed rain away from sleeping area.

SNOW SHELTER (QUINZHEE)
Effective in deep snow. Interior temperature stays near 0°C regardless of outside temperature.
1. Pile snow into a mound 2 meters high. Let it sinter (harden) for 2 hours.
2. Insert sticks 30cm deep around the mound as thickness guides.
3. Hollow out the interior until you hit the sticks.
4. Poke a ventilation hole through the roof with a pole.
5. Sleep on an elevated snow platform to stay above cold air pooling on the floor.

URBAN EMERGENCY SHELTER
- Interior rooms away from windows offer best protection from wind, rain, and blast.
- Basements provide thermal mass and protection. Check for flooding risk.
- Insulate windows with cardboard, clothing, or newspaper.
- A tent pitched inside a building dramatically increases warmth retention.

BODY HEAT CONSERVATION
- Put on dry insulation before you get cold, not after — it is harder to rewarm.
- Cover head and neck: up to 40% of body heat is lost through the head.
- Keep dry: wet clothing loses 90% of its insulating value.
- Huddle with others to share body heat when possible.
